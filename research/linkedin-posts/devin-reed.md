# Post 1:
Title: 5 Post Types to Boost LinkedIn Impressions    
URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/devinreed_breakout-posts-arent-luck-theyre-inevitable-activity-7437553867564662784-b_06  
Date: 2026 

## Summary

In this post, Devin Reed argues that breakout posts on LinkedIn are not a matter of luck — they are inevitable when using proven, repeatable formats. He shares five specific post types that helped a client go from 0 to 89,400 impressions in their first 30 days.

The core idea:
> 👉 Breakout content is repeatable once you understand what works

## Key Insights
- **1st person stories with hard-earned lessons** – The best performing hook. Focus on real moments that taught something worth sharing, not generic “grind mode” narratives.
- **Contrarian takes (without being a try-hard)** – Challenge common consensus only when you genuinely believe it and can back it up. Healthy disagreement drives engagement.
- **Data + what it means** – Numbers provide instant credibility, but the real value comes from adding perspective and explaining why the data matters now.
- **News: what just happened + so what?** – Speed matters, but the “so what” is where value lives. Don’t just report — interpret and help your audience make sense of their world.
- **Deep-dive how-to posts** – Still highly effective when done with nuance. Give people something screenshot-worthy and saveable.

## Why it matters
This insight shifts the focus from hoping for viral moments to building a repeatable content system. For LinkedIn strategy, this means:
- Using proven formats instead of guessing what will work
- Adding interpretation and perspective — not just information
- Designing content to be saved, shared, and engaged with intentionally

## How to use this in the playbook

### Section: “LinkedIn Content Framework”
- Rotate between the 5 post types to maintain variety while relying on proven formats
- Always pair data or news with a clear “so what” — interpretation is the differentiator
- Use contrarian takes strategically: only when you have genuine conviction and evidence

### Section: “Engagement-First Content Design”
- Choose one of the 5 formats as the structural foundation
- Lead with a hook that signals the format (e.g., a personal story, a surprising data point, a contrarian claim)
- Deliver the insight, interpretation, or lesson
- End with a clear pattern or takeaway worth saving

# Post 2:
Title: Challenging the Marketing Funnel: A New Approach to Content Creation   
URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/devinreed_i-once-presented-a-content-plan-to-the-c-suite-activity-7407108176371916800-oFZ4  
Date: 2025 

## Summary

In this post, Devin Reed challenges the traditional marketing funnel (TOFU/MOFU/BOFU), arguing that it no longer reflects how people actually buy. He proposes replacing it with Eugene Schwartz’s 5 Stages of Awareness as a more effective framework for content creation.

The core idea:
> 👉 People move through awareness stages, not funnels — and content must align with where the buyer is mentally

## Key Insights
- The traditional funnel is a helpful starting place but isn’t built for influencing people.
- No buyer thinks in funnel stages (e.g., “I’m feeling very TOFU today”).
- Eugene Schwartz’s 5 Stages of Awareness provide a better framework:
  1. **Unaware** – Doesn’t know they have a problem
  2. **Problem Aware** – Knows the problem but not the solution
  3. **Solution Aware** – Knows solutions exist but not your product
  4. **Product Aware** – Knows your product but isn’t convinced
  5. **Most Aware** – Ready to buy
- Mapping content to these stages prevents mismatched messaging (e.g., promoting features to unaware audiences or over-educating those ready to buy).

## Why it matters
This insight shifts content strategy from a linear, marketer-centric model to a buyer-centric one. For LinkedIn strategy, this means:
- Creating content that meets people where they are in their awareness journey
- Avoiding the trap of building content calendars for internal structure rather than audience mindset
- Treating awareness as a dynamic spectrum, not a static funnel

## How to use this in the playbook

### Section: “Audience-First Content Framework”
- Replace “TOFU/MOFU/BOFU” labels in content calendars with the 5 Stages of Awareness
- Audit existing content: does it speak to the right awareness stage?
- Design content sequences that move people from unaware → most aware

### Section: “Content Mapping Framework”
1. **Unaware** – Problem-discovery content (e.g., “Did you know this hidden cost exists?”)
2. **Problem Aware** – Problem-framing content (e.g., “Why this problem is costing you”)
3. **Solution Aware** – Educational content on solution categories (e.g., “How to evaluate options”)
4. **Product Aware** – Differentiation content (e.g., “What makes our approach different”)
5. **Most Aware** – Conversion content (e.g., “Why now is the right time”)
