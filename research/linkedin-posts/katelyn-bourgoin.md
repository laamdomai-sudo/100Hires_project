# Post 1:
- Title: Say One Thing 1,000 Ways – The Authority Flywheel  
- URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/katebour_them-say-one-thing-1000-ways-you-ok-activity-7438206738853462017-Gyln  
- Date: 2026

## Summary

Katelyn Bourgoin addresses the common advice to “say one thing 1,000 ways” – but notes that no one explains *how*. She introduces the **Authority Flywheel**, a repeatable system designed to convert attention into clients and compound authority systematically. At the center is your **ownable idea**, and four content types form the engine: Problem, POV, Promise, Proof.

The core idea:
> 👉 Repetition is the multiplier. Nail your ownable idea, then use the Authority Flywheel to share it relentlessly from different angles

## Key Insights

- **The problem with “stay on topic”** – Talking consistently about a topic that sounds like everyone else keeps you invisible. Buyers are drowning in information; they want a fresh take from someone whose perspective changes how they think.
- **The Authority Flywheel (4 content types):**
  1. **Problem** – Name their struggle (attracts prospects)
  2. **POV** – Share your unique take or approach (reframes how they think)
  3. **Promise** – Show what’s possible (speaks to aspirations)
  4. **Proof** – Demonstrate you delivered (converts)
- **How it spins** – When you get results for a client, you surface new Problem angles, new POVs, and more client wins that become Proof. Each rotation produces raw material for the next.
- **Example: Codie Sanchez** – Her ownable idea: “You can buck the system and build wealth by owning boring businesses.” She can post about pricing, hiring, and loans without feeling scattered – because every topic traces back to that core idea.
- **The deeper problem** – If you don’t know what to say in a way that makes people care, posting more won’t help. Find your center, nail it, then share it relentlessly.

## Why it matters

For B2B LinkedIn content strategy, this reinforces:
- **Consistency is not about repeating topics – it’s about repeating your core idea** from different angles.
- **A content system beats inspiration** – The Authority Flywheel gives you a repeatable framework to generate posts.
- **People buy into your thinking before they buy from you** – Your POV is your competitive advantage.

## How to use this in the playbook

### Section: “Find Your Ownable Idea”
- Define the one belief or frame that makes your approach unique (e.g., “Stop guessing what works – let customer stories guide you”).
- Test it: Can you trace every post back to this idea?

### Section: “The Authority Flywheel Content Mix”
- Rotate through the 4 types:
  - **Problem** – “My client struggled with X…”
  - **POV** – “Most people get Y wrong. Here’s why…”
  - **Promise** – “Imagine what happens when you solve Z…”
  - **Proof** – “Here’s the result we got for a client…”
- Each post reinforces the same mental territory from a different angle.

### Section: “Diagnose Your Content Problem”
- If you’re stuck, ask: “Do I have a consistency problem? Or a content problem? Or do I not know what to say in a way that makes people care?”
- If it’s the latter, stop posting more. Nail your ownable idea first.

### Section: “Use Client Wins to Fuel the Flywheel”
- When you get a result, extract:
  - New Problem angles (what was the client struggling with?)
  - New POVs (what unique approach solved it?)
  - New Proof (the result itself)

# Post 2:
- Title: Why Is Convincing People to Buy Stuff So Hard?    
- URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/katebour_why-is-convincing-people-to-buy-stuff-so-activity-7265012616475852801-6TEQ  
- Date: 2-25

## Summary

In this post, Katelyn Bourgoin explains why most persuasion efforts fail: marketers try to convince prospects *before* properly pre‑suading them. She shares her own mistake of spending weeks on ads and sales pages without results, until she read Robert Cialdini’s *Pre‑suasion*. The key insight is that effective marketing primes people to be receptive to a pitch *before* they hear it – by setting the right context, cues, and emotions.

The core idea:
> 👉 Pre‑suasion is subtle. Prime your audience before you pitch. Small cues (like a LinkedIn banner) can dramatically shift receptivity.

## Key Insights

- **Pre‑suasion** – Cialdini’s concept: strategic effort to prime people so they’re more receptive to your message *before* you deliver it.
- **Common mistake** – Most marketers focus on persuasion (the pitch) without priming the audience first.
- **Example** – Katelyn updated her LinkedIn banner image before a course launch. This is priming in action.
- **Result** – Using pre‑suasion techniques, her PAINKILLER course pre‑order sold out in **53 minutes** ($33,750) with just **1 email and a Google Doc**.

## Why it matters

For B2B LinkedIn content strategy, this reinforces:
- **Your profile (banner, headline, featured links) is a pre‑suasion tool** – It primes visitors before they ever read a post.
- **Small, consistent cues build receptivity** – A banner that signals authority or a specific outcome sets the stage for your pitch.
- **Pre‑suasion reduces resistance** – When people are already primed, they need less convincing.

## How to use this in the playbook

### Section: “Pre‑suasion on Your LinkedIn Profile”
- **Banner image** – Use it to prime your ICP (e.g., “Stop guessing what works” or a visual of the outcome they want).
- **Headline** – Answer “What will I get by following you?” before they read a single post.
- **Featured links** – Place case studies or lead magnets as subtle cues of credibility.

### Section: “Pre‑suasion in Content”
- Before pitching a product or service, publish 3–5 posts that prime the problem you solve.
- Use consistent visual cues (colors, imagery) across posts to build subconscious association.
- End a pre‑suasion post with a low‑friction CTA (e.g., “Comment ‘access’ for my free email course”).

### Section: “Launch with Pre‑suasion”
- Don’t start with a sales pitch. Start with priming content 1–2 weeks before.
- Update your banner and pinned post to signal the upcoming launch.
- Use a single email + simple page (Google Doc) – the pre‑suasion does the heavy lifting.

# Post 3:
- Title: I Made $0 on My First Launch  
- URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/katebour_i-made-0-on-my-first-launch-and-id-done-activity-7406685176128233472-Fauv  
- Date: 2026

## Summary

Katelyn shares her painful first launch: $0 after 7 weeks of work using “proven” guru templates. The problem wasn’t effort – it was timing. She was persuading without pre‑suading. After reading Cialdini’s *Pre‑suasion*, she learned to prime her audience before the pitch. The result: subsequent launches sold out in minutes and generated six figures.

The core idea:
👉 **Don’t persuade first. Pre‑suade first. Prime your audience before you ever make an offer.**

## Key Insights

- **First launch failure** – $0 revenue despite using the same templates as successful gurus. The missing piece was pre‑suasion.
- **What changed** – After learning to prime people *before* the pitch, results flipped:
  - UNIGNORABLE cohort sold out in **6 minutes** ($119,800)
  - $37,450 from **2 emails + a Google Doc**
  - Built a **7‑figure online business**
- **Pre‑suasion works** – Setting context, cues, and emotions ahead of time makes the offer feel obvious, not pushy.
- **Holiday gift** – She offered a free 6‑day “Pre‑sell Anything” email challenge to teach the framework.

## Why it matters

For B2B LinkedIn content strategy, this reinforces:
- **Your daily content is pre‑suasion** – Every post primes your audience for your next offer.
- **Don’t launch cold** – Warm up your audience with problem‑focused content for 1–2 weeks before pitching.
- **Small audience + pre‑suasion > big audience + cold pitch** – $37k from 2 emails proves it’s about timing and priming, not list size.

## How to use this in the playbook

### Section: “Launch Pre‑suasion Sequence”
- **Week 1–2** – Post content about the problem (not your solution). Use stories, data, and hot takes.
- **Week 3** – Introduce the offer as the natural answer to the problem already primed.
- **Keep it simple** – A Google Doc and 2 emails can outsell a fancy sales page if pre‑suasion is done right.

### Section: “Signal Pre‑suasion on Your Profile”
- Update your banner and pinned post 1 week before a launch to subtly signal what’s coming.
- Use consistent language across posts to build subconscious association.

### Section: “Learn from Failure”
- If a launch flops, don’t blame the offer. Audit your pre‑suasion: Did you prime the problem enough?
- Templates work only when the audience is already pre‑suaded. Otherwise, they fall flat.
