# Post 1:
- Title: I Wrote 610 Hooks for My LinkedIn Posts in 2025 — Here’s My Process    
- URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mattjbarker1_i-wrote-610-hooks-for-my-linkedin-posts-in-activity-7444352632158150658-mWIx  
- Date: 2026

## Summary

In this post, Matt Barker shares the exact process he used to write 610 hooks for LinkedIn posts in 2025. Instead of relying on viral formulas or AI-generated openers, he advocates for a simple, human-first approach rooted in lived experience.

The core idea:
> 👉 Stop trying to sound clever. Start with something real that happened to you.

## Key Insights

- Step 1: Pick a recent lived experience – Look back at the last 7 days for calls, arguments, outcomes, wins, or fails. Anything you can say “this happened.”
- Step 2: Write it out like a text message – Don’t over-hookify it. Explain what happened naturally, just as you would to a friend over coffee.
- Step 3: Refine it – Check for elements like timeline, location, actions, and stakes. Ensure the hook creates curiosity. Ask yourself: “Can anyone else write this?”
- Top 5 hooks from 2025 (provided as examples) – Each is specific, personal, and creates a natural curiosity gap.
- The “can anyone else write this?” test – If the answer is yes, the hook lacks specificity and personal voice.

## Why it matters

This post challenges the trend of formulaic, AI-generated hooks that dominate LinkedIn. It reinforces that:
- Specificity beats cleverness – Real moments create natural curiosity.
- Authenticity is a competitive advantage – When AI can generate endless hooks, human-lived experience becomes the differentiator.
- Process matters – A simple, repeatable system (look back → write naturally → refine) replaces guesswork.

## How to use this in the playbook

### Section: “Hook Writing System”
1. Mine lived experiences – Weekly review of calls, wins, fails, client moments, arguments.
2. First draft: text message mode – Write like you’re explaining to a friend.
3. Refine for curiosity – Add timeline, location, stakes. Create a gap the reader wants to close.

### Section: “The Specificity Test”
- Before publishing, ask: “Could anyone else have written this?”
- If yes → add more specific details (numbers, names, context, personal reaction).
- Use the top 5 hooks as inspiration, but insert your own lived experience.

# Post 2:

- Title: I Posted the Same LinkedIn Post 10 Weeks in a Row — Then One Week It Flopped    
- URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mattjbarker1_i-posted-the-same-linkedin-post-10-weeks-activity-7440743589141409792-g304  
- Date: 2026  

## Summary

In this post, Matt Barker shares a counterintuitive lesson from his content system: after reposting the same successful post for 10 weeks, it finally underperformed. Instead of panicking, changing strategy, or deleting the post, he decided to do nothing.

The core idea:
> 👉 One bad post is not a pattern. Don’t let a single data point torch a proven system.

## Key Insights

- One post does not make a trend – With 9 months of data showing consistent performance, one underperforming week is noise, not signal.
- Don’t scrap the strategy over a single data point – The instinct to reinvent everything after one failure kills momentum.
- Reposting works – Reposting timeless content reaches a new audience each time. Most followers won’t remember seeing it before.
- Emotional consistency is a competitive advantage – The writers who grow aren’t the ones who never have a bad post; they’re the ones who don’t let a bad post send them spiraling.
- Data requires sample size – Analyze over 20–30 posts, not one. Small samples lead to overreaction.

## Why it matters

This post challenges the common impulse to over-optimize based on short-term performance. For B2B content strategy, it reinforces:
- Trust your system over your feelings – Proven formats and topics should survive occasional underperformance.
- Reposting is not “lazy” – It’s a deliberate strategy to reach net-new audiences with high-value content.
- Emotional discipline matters – Consistency in execution compounds more than chasing every algorithm fluctuation.

## How to use this in the playbook

### Section: “Content Reposting Strategy”
- Identify timeless, high-performing posts (e.g., revenue posts, tool posts, one-person business insights)
- Repost on a regular cadence (weekly, bi-weekly) knowing each repost reaches a fresh segment of followers
- Accept that individual posts will vary in performance; measure over 20–30 reposts, not one

### Section: “Data-Driven Decision Making”
- Set a minimum sample size (e.g., 20 posts) before evaluating a format or topic
- Distinguish between signal (consistent patterns over time) and noise (single-week fluctuations)
- When one post flops: do nothing. Let the data accumulate.

### Section: “Emotional Consistency for Creators”
- Separate self-worth from post performance
- Build systems that work over months, not days
- Remember: the goal is compound growth, not a perfect hit rate

# Post 3:

- Title:Reverse Engineering My Best Performing LinkedIn Post

- Date: 2026 

## Summary

In this post, Matt Barker reverse-engineers his best-performing LinkedIn post from March to understand exactly why it worked. The post achieved 21k impressions (nearly 2x average), 148 saves, and 45 link clicks. He breaks down the elements that made it successful and shares a repeatable framework.

The core idea:
> 👉 Don’t “come up with ideas” — document things. Everything you do has a LinkedIn post in it.

## Key Insights

**What made the post work (5 elements):**
1. Hook placed the reader in lived experience – Included timeline, location, character, action, and stakes. Nothing extraordinary, but interesting enough to lean in.
2. Only I could have written it – Personal fingerprint (specific Zoom call, specific advice) made it feel real and irreplaceable.
3. Curiosity gap done right – Gained reader investment *first*, then opened the loop. Most people create the cliffhanger before the reader cares.
4. Lesson instantly usable – Something readers could apply in the next 5 minutes. Useful things get saved; saved posts drive impressions.
5. Ending short and simple – One line summarizing the lesson. Memorable and easy to digest.

**The exact framework (Scene → Problem → One-word fix → Example → Why it works → Proof → Lesson → CTA)**

## Why it matters

This post provides a practical, repeatable structure for turning everyday experiences into high-performing content. For B2B LinkedIn strategy, it reinforces:
- Document, don’t create – Content ideas already exist in your daily work (calls, advice given, problems solved).
- Personal fingerprint is a competitive advantage – If anyone else could have written it, it’s not differentiated.
- Saves are a key metric – Posts that get saved signal high utility to the algorithm, driving more impressions.
- Curiosity gap requires context first – Make readers care before you leave them hanging.

## How to use this in the playbook

### Section: “Documenting Lived Experience”
- Instead of brainstorming topics, look at your last 7 days: what calls did you have? What advice did you give? What problem did you solve?
- Write posts that capture specific moments with timeline, location, character, action, and stakes.

### Section: “Post Structure Framework”
Use the 8-part framework:
1. **Scene** – Set the context (lived experience)
2. **Problem** – What was the challenge?
3. **One-word fix** – The core insight distilled
4. **Example** – Specific illustration
5. **Why it works** – Rationale
6. **Proof** – Evidence or result
7. **Lesson** – Takeaway for reader
8. **CTA** – Clear next step

### Section: “Curiosity Gap Checklist”
- Does the reader care about the topic before the cliffhanger?
- Is the hook grounded in specific detail (who, what, when, where)?
- Does the post deliver on the curiosity (not just clickbait)?

### Section: “Metrics That Matter”
- Track **saves** as a signal of utility
- Monitor **link clicks** for intent
- Use **impressions** as a secondary measure (saves often drive them)
