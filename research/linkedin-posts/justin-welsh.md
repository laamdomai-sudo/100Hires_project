# Post 1:
- Title: How I Gained 675K+ LinkedIn Followers    
- URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justinwelsh_how-i-gained-675k-linkedin-followers-activity-7273019380517552129-eTn_  
- *Date: 2024

## Summary

In this post, Justin Welsh reflects on his LinkedIn journey—from writing his first post in 2018 with no fancy equipment or editing skills to building a following of over 675,000. He argues that betting on yourself is not risky; the downside is limited, while the upside is unlimited. LinkedIn, despite its growth, remains the most undervalued platform for professionals.

The core idea:
> 👉 The downside of betting on yourself is limited. The upside is absolutely unlimited.

## Key Insights

- **The risk calculation is misunderstood** – People think betting on yourself is risky, but the opposite is true. The potential downside is small (time, effort), while the upside is unlimited (career, income, influence).
- **LinkedIn is still undervalued** – Despite its growth, the platform remains underutilized for what it offers: access to decision‑makers and professionals.
- **What you need is simple** – Clarity about the problem you solve, consistency in showing up, and courage to start. No fancy equipment or design skills required.
- **Personal example** – Justin started with just his knowledge and text. That was enough to create life‑changing results.

## Why it matters

This post is a foundational reminder for anyone hesitant to start creating content. For B2B creators, it reinforces:
- **The barrier to entry is low** – You don’t need production value; you need clarity and consistency.
- **LinkedIn’s value persists** – Even as the platform grows, the opportunity to reach decision‑makers remains unparalleled.
- **The biggest risk is not starting** – The cost of inaction is higher than the cost of trying.

## How to use this in the playbook

### Section: “Mindset: Betting on Yourself”
- Reframe risk: the downside is time and effort; the upside is unlimited.
- Start before you feel ready. Skills and polish come with reps, not before them.
- Use LinkedIn as the primary platform because it gives direct access to your ICP.

### Section: “The Three Essentials”
1. **Clarity** – Know the problem you solve and who you solve it for.
2. **Consistency** – Show up on a regular cadence; compound growth requires sustained effort.
3. **Courage** – Start. Post. Learn. Iterate.

# Post 2:
- Title: The Content Game Isn’t About Being Original — It’s About Being Useful    
- URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justinwelsh_the-content-game-isnt-about-being-original-activity-7362677566433816576-Hzsn  
- Date: 2025

## Summary

In this post, Justin Welsh challenges the pressure to be constantly original. He argues that the smartest content strategy is to find 1,000 ways to say the same thing. His entire business—4,700+ posts, $10.5M+ in revenue—is built on just 5 core ideas repeated in different angles, stories, and packaging.

The core idea:
> 👉 Repetition isn’t boring. It’s branding.

## Key Insights

- **Burnout comes from chasing originality** – Most creators exhaust themselves trying to invent something new daily. The pros find one truth and explore it deeply.
- **Justin’s 5 core ideas:**
  1. Content is currency
  2. Systems beat hustle
  3. Leverage your expertise
  4. Life first, business second
  5. One-person businesses can thrive
- **Top creators follow the same pattern** – James Clear (habits), Naval (wealth and happiness). Same themes, fresh packaging, endless value.
- **Your audience doesn’t need variety** – They need you to be focused. Repetition builds recognition, trust, and brand.
- **People remember the one thing you say 100 times** – Not the 100 things you say once.

## Why it matters

This post reframes repetition from a weakness to a strategic advantage. For B2B content creators, it reinforces:
- **Depth > breadth** – Exploring a few core ideas deeply builds authority more effectively than covering many topics shallowly.
- **Consistency compounds** – A focused message repeated over time becomes your brand.
- **Work smarter, not harder** – Instead of reinventing every post, find new angles, stories, and examples for your core themes.

## How to use this in the playbook

### Section: “Find Your 5 Core Ideas”
- Distill your business or expertise down to 3–5 foundational truths.
- Write them down. These become your content pillars.
- Every post should connect back to one of these ideas.

### Section: “1,000 Ways to Say the Same Thing”
- For each core idea, generate variations:
  - Different stories (client wins, personal failures, lessons learned)
  - Different formats (listicles, stories, hot takes, data)
  - Different angles (for beginners, for experts, contrarian)
- Ask: “How can I say this idea in a new way today?”

### Section: “Repetition as Branding”
- Embrace that your audience hears your message many times before it sticks.
- Don’t worry about boring existing followers—new people join constantly, and familiar messages build trust.
- Measure success by how well your audience can repeat your core ideas back to you.

# Post 3:

- Title: My Strategy When I Started Was Pretty Basic  
- URL: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justinwelsh_my-strategy-when-i-started-was-pretty-basic-activity-7415011488983203840-7Rmy  
- Date: 2026

## Summary

In this post, Justin Welsh reflects on how his strategy when starting on LinkedIn was refreshingly simple: write stuff online, see what worked, do more of that, do less of what didn’t. No complicated funnel, no 90-day calendar, no brand positioning document. Six years later, that basic approach built an eight‑figure business.

The core idea:
> 👉 A decent plan executed well will beat a brilliant plan executed occasionally. Every single time.

## Key Insights

- **Simple strategy, consistent execution** – Justin’s approach was: show up, put things out there, pay attention. That’s it.
- **Over‑optimization is a trap** – He’s watched people with “better” strategies—perfect branding, beautiful websites, 19‑step funnels—go nowhere because they never took action.
- **The strategy almost never matters as much as you think** – What matters is doing the work consistently, even when it’s not very good yet.
- **Movement creates clarity** – You don’t need clarity to start. Starting creates clarity.
- **Execution compounds** – A basic plan executed consistently beats a brilliant plan executed occasionally.

## Why it matters

This post is a direct challenge to the “strategy porn” that paralyzes many creators. For B2B content professionals, it reinforces:
- **Action > planning** – Perfecting the roadmap delays the real work.
- **Start before you’re ready** – You learn more from publishing than from planning.
- **Simplicity scales** – You don’t need a complex system to start; you build the system as you go.

## How to use this in the playbook

### Section: “The Minimum Viable Content Strategy”
- Start with one rule: write, publish, see what works, do more of that.
- No complicated calendar required. Just show up consistently.
- Use feedback (engagement, DMs, leads) as your guide for what to double down on.

### Section: “Action Over Optimization”
- Set a deadline to start, not to finish planning.
- Limit strategy work to 20% of your time; spend 80% on execution.
- If you’re spending more time on tools, templates, or positioning than on writing, pause and just publish.

### Section: “The Execution Mindset”
- Embrace that early work will be imperfect.
- Let the market teach you what works—don’t guess.
- Consistency beats brilliance. Show up, learn, adjust.
